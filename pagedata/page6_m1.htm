Mentoring Moment: Approaches for Negative Messages

It's never easy to deliver bad news. Whether you're telling an employee he or she is being fired or laid off; providing constructive criticism on job performance; or, as in this scenario, telling a team their prototype isn't going into production, how you choose to deliver the message will influence its response.

Let's look at some reasons why you'd want to use a direct approach or an indirect approach.

Direct Strategy Indirect Strategy
Use a direct strategy to deliver bad news when: Use an indirect strategy to deliver bad news when:
The situation is an emergency or the news is critically important. The issue is very important to the receiver but not an emergency.
The bad news doesn't personally affect the receiver. The bad news is expected to be personally upsetting to the receiver.
The bad news is expected. The bad news is unexpected.
The bad news is easy to explain and understand. The bad news is difficult to understand without a lot of explanation.
The receiver is unlikely to become upset or angry. The bad news will likely provoke a hostile reaction.
A good working relationship with the receiver is unimportant A good working relationship with the receiver is important.
